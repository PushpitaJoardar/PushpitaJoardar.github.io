Multi-Level Feature Fusion: A Hybrid CLIP & Wavelet Pipeline for Robust Synthetic Image Detection

2025

This research proposes a hybrid framework for detecting synthetic images generated by diffusion and GAN-based models by fusing high-level semantic representations with low-level frequency-domain artifacts. A frozen CLIP ViT-B/32 image encoder extracts robust semantic features, while Haar wavelet transforms capture subtle frequency inconsistencies introduced during image generation. The features are combined using cross-attention and a learnable gating mechanism to dynamically balance semantic and spectral information.

Accepted, IEEE 5th International Conference on Computing and Machine Intelligence (ICCMI) 2026

  • Methodology: Fused frozen CLIP ViT-B/32 semantic embeddings with Haar wavelet frequency features using cross-attention and learnable gating.
  • Performance: Achieved 97.30% AUC and 93.78% accuracy in-distribution; demonstrated strong zero-shot out-of-distribution performance with 97.36% AUC and 88.28% accuracy.
  • Evaluation: Benchmarked against CLIP LAT10k+, DeeCLIP, Wavelet-CLIP, RINE, and Co-Spy, with extensive ablation studies validating the contributions of fusion and contrastive learning.
Coordinated Agent Movement Analysis under IARPA HAYSTAC

2025 – Present

This research focuses on identifying coordinated anomalous movements among multiple agents using large-scale spatiotemporal trajectory data under the IARPA HAYSTAC program. Agent trajectories are discretized into grid-based spatial representations and analyzed over time to uncover coordinated behaviors that deviate from normal patterns of life.

Collaboration with Raytheon Technologies Research Center (RTRC) and University of California, Santa Barbara (UCSB)

  • Detection: Detected coordinated anomalous movements among multiple agents using grid-based spatiotemporal analysis.
  • Patterns: Identified recurring multi-agent co-location events, including long-duration coordinated stops and repeated agent triplets.
  • Analysis: Distinguished routine co-presence from anomalous coordination using temporal binning and calendar-level analysis.
LLM-based Anomalous Agent Behavior Analysis for HAYSTAC

2024 – 2025

This research explores the use of large language models (LLMs) to interpret and explain anomalous human mobility patterns in spatiotemporal trajectory data. Agent behaviors are evaluated against Hypothesis of Significance (HOS) specifications designed to inject task-driven deviations into otherwise routine movement patterns.

IARPA HAYSTAC Program — Collaborative Research with Raytheon Technologies Research Center (RTRC) and UCSB

  • Anomaly Detection: Detected and analyzed anomalous agent behaviors using HAYSTAC HOS specifications.
  • LLM Reasoning: Used LLMs to generate interpretable, paragraph-level explanations linking observed behavior deviations to task-driven anomaly injections.
  • Classification: Categorized anomalies based on spatial novelty, temporal deviation, and routine disruption severity.
